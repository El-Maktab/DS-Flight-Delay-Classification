\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Abstract}}

Multi-class classification model to predict flight departure delay severity before pushback. Data sources: 2015 BTS domestic flight records (CSV) + Open-Meteo Historical Weather API. Four classes: \textbf{On Time}, \textbf{Minor Delay}, \textbf{Major Delay}, \textbf{Cancelled}. Final dataset: 116,381 rows $\times$ 55 columns. Validation found no full-row duplicates and only expected missing-value patterns.

% ════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Problem Statement}}

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{2.1 Business Problem}}

Flight delays cause operational disruptions, increased costs, and poor passenger experience. The goal is to \textbf{predict departure delay severity before pushback}, enabling ground operations to take proportional action in advance.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{2.2 Stakeholders}}

\textbf{Primary:} Airline operations team and airport management.

\textbf{Secondary:} Passengers and customer service teams.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{2.3 Why Classification}}

Delay severity is a discrete outcome, not a continuous one. Each class maps to a distinct operational response, making classification the best option to use. Multi-class over binary captures severity categories. Misclassification costs are asymmetric, missing a major delay is worse than over-alerting, which is handled through business-oriented evaluation metrics.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{2.4 Decision-Making Impact}}

\vspace{0.3em}

\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Target Variable Definition}}

The classification target is derived from the \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} column (minutes) and the \code{CANCELLED} flag.

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{tcolorbox}[mainbox, title={Class Distribution}]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{2.5cm} L{2.8cm} X C{1.8cm} C{1.2cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Class} & \textbf{Label} & \textbf{Definition} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{\%} \\
    \midrule
    \code{on\_time}     & On Time     & \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} $\leq$ 15 min (FAA standard) & 94,345 & 81.1\% \\
    \code{minor\_delay} & Minor Delay & $15 <$ \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} $\leq$ 45 min          & 11,433 &  9.8\% \\
    \code{major\_delay} & Major Delay & \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} $>$ 45 min                    &  8,833 &  7.6\% \\
    \code{cancelled}    & Cancelled   & \code{CANCELLED == 1}                                  &  1,770 &  1.5\% \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Why \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY}:} The goal is pre-departure action, departure delay is the directly actionable outcome.

\textbf{Class thresholds:} The 15-minute threshold follows the FAA on-time standard. The 45-minute split is where airlines typically begin rebooking connecting passengers.

\textbf{Cancelled flights:} Retained as a distinct fourth class. They have no \code{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} but cancellation is itself a severe outcome. Final handling deferred to the preprocessing phase.

\textbf{Class imbalance:} \code{on\_time} dominates at 81.1\%. Will be addressed using \textbf{SMOTE and other techniques while training}.

\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Merge Strategy}}

\textbf{Type:} Horizontal merge, weather columns are appended to each flight row.

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{tcolorbox}[mainbox, title={Join Keys}]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{3cm} X L{4.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Join} & \textbf{Key} & \textbf{Weather Columns Added} \\
    \midrule
    Origin weather      & \code{(ORIGIN\_AIRPORT, MONTH, DAY, departure\_hour)}                    & 11 cols (\code{temperature\_c}, \ldots) \\
    Destination weather & \code{(DESTINATION\_AIRPORT, MONTH, arrival\_day, scheduled\_arrival\_hour)} & 11 cols (\code{dest\_temperature\_c}, \ldots) \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Timezone handling:} The Open-Meteo API returns timestamps in \code{America/New\_York}. Flight schedules are in each airport's local time. \code{timezonefinder} resolves each airport's IANA timezone from its coordinates, \code{zoneinfo} converts local departure/arrival hours to Eastern before building the lookup key, correctly handling DST for all 2015 dates.

\textbf{Overnight flights:} Arrival day is computed by adding \code{SCHEDULED\_TIME} (minutes) to departure hour, detecting day rollovers where the flight arrives the next calendar day.

\vspace{0.3em}
\begin{tcolorbox}[mainbox, title={Row Counts Before/After Merging}]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{X R{3.5cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Row Count} \\
    \midrule
    Raw BTS flights CSV                                           & $\sim$5,819,079 \\
    After 2\% stratified sample (by month)                        & 116,381 \\
    After weather join (no rows dropped)                          & 116,381 \\
    Rows with missing origin weather (numeric airport codes)       & 9,837 \enspace(8.45\%) \\
    Rows with missing destination weather (numeric airport codes)  & 9,823 \enspace(8.44\%) \\
    \textbf{Final dataset}                                        & \textbf{116,381 $\times$ 55 cols} \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[quotebox]
The join does not drop rows, flights with no valid airport code get \code{NaN} weather values. These $\sim$9,837 rows will be dropped in the cleaning phase as weather is a core feature set.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{8.2 Target Variable Creation}}
The primary objective of the classification model is to predict the severity of flight delays. A new target variable, \texttt{DELAY\_CATEGORY}, was engineered based on the continuous \texttt{DEPARTURE\_DELAY} and the \texttt{CANCELLED} binary indicator:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{On Time:} Flights departing up to 15 minutes after the scheduled time (adhering to the standard FAA definition of on-time performance).
    \item \textbf{Minor Delay:} Flights delayed between 16 and 45 minutes.
    \item \textbf{Major Delay:} Flights delayed for more than 45 minutes.
    \item \textbf{Cancelled:} Flights explicitly marked as cancelled in the original dataset.
\end{itemize}
Any rows lacking sufficient data to be categorized into one of these four discrete classes were subsequently dropped.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{8.3 Data Cleaning and Filtering}}
Several data quality checks and filtering mechanisms were applied to ensure the integrity of the feature space:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Invalid Airport Codes:} Records containing numeric IATA airport codes (e.g., \texttt{12345}) were discarded, as they indicate corrupted or incorrectly mapped origin or destination data.
    \item \textbf{Null Handling:} Any residual rows containing null values in mandatory feature columns were removed to ensure dataset completeness. Exceptions were made for conditionally null columns such as \texttt{CANCELLATION\_REASON}, which are logically empty for completed flights.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{8.4 Outlier Treatment}}
To improve the robustness of downstream machine learning models against extreme, unrepresentative values, statistical outlier capping was performed. Continuous numeric features-such as \texttt{DISTANCE}, \texttt{SCHEDULED\_TIME}, and weather-related metrics (\texttt{temperature\_c}, \texttt{wind\_speed\_kmh})-were capped at their 1st and 99th percentiles.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{8.5 Post-Cleaning Validation}}
As a final step, the preprocessed dataset was automatically passed through a secondary validation suite. This ensured that no residual nulls existed in the feature space and that all values adhered to expected logical ranges before the data was serialized and passed to the feature engineering phase.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{9.1 Feature selection tried}} 

We also tried a small \textbf{filter-based feature-selection step} during training. The method was \textbf{mutual information}: each feature was scored against the training labels, and only columns above a minimum threshold were kept. In code, this was implemented as an optional \code{mutual\_info} mode with a default \code{min\_mutual\_info} threshold of \code{0.001}. If no column passed the threshold, the pipeline kept the single best varying feature as a fallback.

We kept this as an experiment rather than making it part of the final default pipeline. In the ablation runs, the mutual-information filter gave only negligible changes and did not consistently improve test performance. For example, HistGradientBoosting stayed slightly better without it (balanced accuracy about \textbf{0.378} without selection vs \textbf{0.373} with mutual information), while balanced logistic regression changed only marginally (about \textbf{0.396} without selection vs \textbf{0.397} with it). Because the gain was small and inconsistent, the final system relied more on \textbf{feature engineering plus model-specific feature adaptation} than on aggressive column pruning.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{11.3 Training, Validation, and Tuning Strategy}}

\begin{itemize}[label=\textcolor{accentblue}{\textbullet}]
    \item \textbf{Train/Testing split:} the cleaned dataset was split with a class-stratified 80/20 way. The winning final run used \textbf{85,154 training rows}, \textbf{21,290 holdout rows}, and \textbf{61 feature columns}.
    \item \textbf{Feature generation:} every model used the same core feature set: temporal features, route context, congestion proxies, weather intensity, frequency encodings, and train-only smoothed historical rates.
    \item \textbf{Leakage control:} the train-only encodings were fit on the training partition and then applied to validation or holdout data with smoothing and fallbacks for unseen groups.
    \item \textbf{Model-specific adaptation:} the histogram boosting models kept collapsing toward the majority class when the dense \code{\_historical\_} target-encoding columns stayed in the matrix. We removed those columns for \code{hist\_gradient\_boosting} and \code{hierarchical\_hist\_gradient\_boosting}, and that fixed the issue.
    \item \textbf{Cross-validation:} inside training, we used stratified cross-validation to estimate generalization without touching the final testing set.
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter tuning:} randomized CV search was implemented on best models.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{11.4 Final Model-Selection Rule}}

Models were ranked by \textbf{balanced accuracy}, then tie-broken by \textbf{macro F1}, \textbf{average misclassification cost}, \textbf{cost reduction vs the on-time baseline}, and \textbf{weighted F1}. In practice, that meant we preferred models that still did useful work on the minority classes instead of just inflating overall accuracy.

More on this part in the results and evaluation section.

\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Results \& Evaluation}}

The final evaluation used technical metrics and business metrics side by side. We kept the usual classification scores, but we did not want the report to stop at them because this is an imbalanced problem and some mistakes matter much more than others. On the business side, we used a simple misclassification-cost scheme that makes missed disruption events more expensive than false alarms

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{14.0 Why These Metrics Were Used}}

\begin{tcolorbox}[mainbox, title={What Each Metric Tells Us}]
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.35}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{L{3.1cm} L{4.3cm} X}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Why We Used It} & \textbf{Business Meaning} \\
    \midrule
    Accuracy & We kept accuracy because it is the easiest overall number to read and compare. & - \\
    Balanced accuracy & This was the \textbf{main ranking metric}. It averages recall across the four classes, so every class counts equally. &- \\
    Macro F1 & We used macro F1 to balance precision and recall while still giving equal weight to each class. & - \\
    Weighted F1 & We kept weighted F1 as a secondary summary because it follows the real class mix in the data. & - \\
    Per-class precision, recall, and F1 & We reported the class-wise scores for the best-model review and the error analysis. & - \\
    Average misclassification cost & This was the \textbf{main business metric}. It assigns a penalty to each wrong prediction and averages that cost over all flights. Lower is better. & It turns model errors into something closer to operational impact. Missing a cancelled flight hurts much more than falsely warning on a normal flight. \\
    Cost reduction vs on-time baseline & We compared every model with a naive rule that predicts every flight as \code{on\_time}. Higher is better. & A positive value means the model saves expected business cost. A zero or negative value means the model is not helping enough to justify using it. \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

The business-cost weights were chosen to match what these mistakes mean in operations. A false alarm on an \code{on\_time} flight has a small cost because it may trigger extra monitoring or passenger messages, but it usually does not disrupt the day badly. Missing a \code{minor\_delay} is more expensive because updates start late and connection handling gets weaker. Missing a \code{major\_delay} is worse. Missing a \code{cancelled} flight has the highest cost because the airline loses precious time on the most urgent disruption response.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{14.3 Error Analysis and Business Interpretation}}

Most of the mistakes came from \textbf{over-predicting disruption} rather than missing every disruption case entirely. The three most common confusion pairs were:

\begin{itemize}[label=\textcolor{accentblue}{\textbullet}]
    \item \code{on\_time \rightarrow minor delay}
    \item \code{on\_time \rightarrow major delay}
    \item \code{on\_time \rightarrow cancelled}
\end{itemize}

For the stakeholder, this means the model is \textbf{useful for pre-departure prioritization}. It catches a meaningful share of the major disruption cases and reduces cost relative to an \code{all-on-time} rule. Still, it should be treated as a \textbf{decision-support signal}, not as a fully automated trigger, because the false-positive rate on nominally on-time flights is still high.

\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Code Automation}}

The project was structured so that the main workflow can be reproduced from the command line without manually editing notebooks or changing paths inside the code. We kept the automation simple on purpose: each stage of the pipeline is exposed either as a \textbf{Make target} or as a \textbf{Python CLI entrypoint}.

\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{15.1 Makefile-Based Pipeline Automation}}

The first automation layer is the project \code{Makefile}. It defines the common development commands used throughout the semester, including environment setup, linting, formatting, testing, and the main data-to-model pipeline. The project rules are chained in dependency order, so commands such as \code{make train} or \code{make evaluate} automatically execute the upstream steps first. In practice, that means the workflow moves in a consistent sequence:

\begin{center}
\code{requirements $\rightarrow$ data $\rightarrow$ features $\rightarrow$ train $\rightarrow$ evaluate}
\end{center}

The same Makefile also includes \code{make lint}, \code{make format}, \code{make test}, and \code{make mlflow-ui}, so local quality checks and experiment inspection use the same interface as the modeling steps instead of relying on ad hoc terminal commands.

\section{\textcolor{accentblue}{Limitations \& Future Work}}


\subsection*{\textcolor{accentgold}{17.1 Current Limitations}}

\begin{itemize}[label=\textcolor{accentblue}{\textbullet}]
    \item \textbf{Low mutual-information for pre-departure features}: we found that the notebooks that use this datasets have huge data leakage so they get high results but the classification task itself without the leaking columns is a hard task even when augmented with weather data.
    \item \textbf{The weather data is historical not forecast}
    \item \textbf{The data is scoped in one year only}
    \item \textbf{The missing tail\_number (plane\_identifier) reduced feature engineering} Tail number could have been used to track the cascading singal of a plane that has a delay in a certain day.
\end{itemize}


To conclude, the current system is already strong enough to support \textbf{prioritization and early warning}, but not strong enough for full automation in an airline.
